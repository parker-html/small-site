KILLER WHALE

The killer whale or orca (Orcinus orca) is a toothed whale belonging to the oceanic dolphin family, of which it is the largest member. Killer whales have a diverse diet, although individual populations often specialize in particular types of prey. Some feed exclusively on fish, while others hunt marine mammals such as seals and other species of dolphin. They have been known to attack baleen whale calves, and even adult whales. Killer whales are apex predators, as no animal preys on them. A cosmopolitan species, they can be found in each of the worldâ€™s oceans in a variety of marine environments, from Arctic and Antarctic regions to tropical seas, absent only from the Baltic and Black seas, and some areas of the Arctic Ocean. Killer whales are highly social; some populations are composed of matrilineal family groups (pods) which are the most stable of any animal species. Their sophisticated hunting techniques and vocal behaviours, which are often specific to a particular group and passed across generations, have been described as manifestations of animal culture.

Taxonomy

Orcinus orca is the only recognized extant species in the genus Orcinus, and one of many animal species originally described by Linnaeus in 1758 in Systema Naturae.[6] Konrad Gessner wrote the first scientific description of a killer whale in his Piscium & aquatilium animantium natura of 1558, part of the larger Historia animalium, based on examination of a dead stranded animal in the Bay of Greifswald that had attracted a great deal of local interest.[7] The killer whale is one of 35 species in the oceanic dolphin family, which first appeared about 11 million years ago. The killer whale lineage probably branched off shortly thereafter.[8] Although it has morphological similarities with the false killer whale, the pygmy killer whale and the pilot whales, a study of cytochrome b gene sequences by Richard LeDuc indicated that its closest extant relatives are the snubfin dolphins of the genus Orcaella.[9] However, a more recent (2018) study places the orca as a sister taxon to the Lissodelphininae, a clade that includes Lagenorhynchus and Cephalorhynchus.[10]

Biology

- Appearance: Jet Black top side with white underbelly - Large dorsal fins, 5.9ft tall. Male fins are straight, females are curved - Size: 16-26ft long, Weight: 3-6 tons - Great eye sight, hearing, and sense of touch - Use echolocation to locate and comunicate

Ecology

Can be found in nearly every ocean across the world, but they tend to prefer costal waters, such as Iceland. Although killer whales have been seen migrating into warmer waters such as the Golf of Mexico. Killer Whales have even washed up in fresh water rivers. The total population is estimated as at least 50,000 Killer Whales as of 2006. But roughly 25,000 whales of total population live in Antarctica.
